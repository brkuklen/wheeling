What is the Wheel?

The wheel is a repeatable two-stage options strategy that generates income from stocks you are willing to own. You collect premium by selling options — first puts, then (if assigned) calls — and recycle the cycle indefinitely.

The core idea in one sentence

Sell a Cash-Secured Put on a stock you'd buy anyway. If it expires worthless, keep the premium and sell another. If you get assigned the shares, sell Covered Calls against them until they're called away. Then start over.

Step 1 — Sell a Cash-Secured Put (CSP)

A put option gives the buyer the right to sell 100 shares at the strike price. When you sell a put, you collect premium in exchange for the obligation to buy 100 shares at the strike if the buyer exercises.

"Cash-secured" means you set aside enough cash to actually buy the shares if assigned. One contract = 100 shares = strike × 100 in collateral.

How to pick the strike

Common rules of thumb (yours appear to favor 5-10 delta or 1-3% OTM, weekly DTE):

  • Delta as probability proxy. A 0.20 delta put has ~20% chance of finishing ITM.
  • % OTM. Pick a strike you'd be genuinely happy owning at.
  • DTE (days to expiration). Shorter = faster theta decay, more frequent trades, more touches.
  • IV rank. Higher IV = fatter premium for the same delta.

Step 2 — The Three Outcomes

By expiration, every short put resolves into exactly one of three states:

EXPIRED Stock stays above strike

The put expires worthless. You keep 100% of the premium and your collateral is released. This is the goal. Sell another one.

Example: NVDA-001 — sold $172.50 put for $0.61, NVDA stayed above, kept $61.

ROLLED Stock drops near or below strike — you defend

Instead of taking assignment, you buy back the current put and sell a new one further out in time (and often lower in strike) for a net credit. This buys you more time and lowers your cost basis if eventually assigned.

See Rolling below for the full mechanic.

ASSIGNED Stock finishes below strike — you buy the shares

You're obligated to buy 100 shares at the strike, regardless of the current market price. Your effective cost basis is strike − premium received. Now you own the stock and move to the covered call phase.

Example: AMD-001 — sold $172.50 put for $1.12, AMD dropped, assigned at effective basis of $171.38/share.

Step 3 — Rolling a Position

A roll is a single combined trade: close the existing short option and open a new one. The new option is usually further dated and may be at a different strike. The goal is to collect a net credit (or break even) while postponing assignment.

When to roll vs accept assignment

  • Roll if you can get a meaningful net credit (typically > $0.10/share) and you don't want the shares yet (capital tied up, better opportunities elsewhere).
  • Accept assignment if rolling gives you a tiny or negative credit, or if you're happy to own at this basis and start the CC phase.

Step 4 — Assignment: Now You Own the Stock

When assigned, your cash collateral is converted into 100 shares per contract at the strike price. Your effective cost basis is:

cost_basis = strike − (total_premium_collected_across_chain / 100)

Cost basis example — AMD-001

strike = $172.50
premium = $1.12/share (single leg, no rolls)
effective basis = 172.50 − 1.12 = $171.38/share

You paid $17,250 cash for 100 AMD shares, but the $112 premium effectively reduces your basis to $17,138. Any CC premium you collect from here lowers it further.

The honest truth about assignment

If you got assigned, the stock has dropped through your strike. You're sitting on an unrealized loss on day one. The wheel works because:

  1. You only sell CSPs on stocks you actually want to own.
  2. Premium collected (CSP + rolls + future CCs) keeps lowering your basis.
  3. You're not forced to sell at a loss — you sell CCs and wait.

Step 5 — Sell a Covered Call (CC)

Now that you own 100 shares, you sell a call option against them. A call gives the buyer the right to buy your shares at the strike. You collect premium in exchange for capping your upside at the strike.

"Covered" means the shares back the obligation — no extra collateral needed.

Picking the call strike

  • Above your cost basis. Critical rule — if called away, you book a profit on shares + premium.
  • OTM by your comfort level. Further OTM = less premium, more upside room.
  • Avoid capping a runner. If you'd hate selling at this strike, don't sell the call.

CC outcomes (mirror the CSP outcomes)

  • EXPIRED Stock stays below strike — keep premium, sell another CC.
  • ROLLED Stock rallies above strike — roll up and out for a credit if you want to keep the shares.
  • CALLED AWAY Stock above strike at expiry — shares sold at strike. Now back to selling CSPs.

The Math That Matters

Per-trade ROI

ROI = premium_received / collateral_required

For a CSP: collateral is strike × 100. For a CC: collateral is the share value (or your cost basis, depending on how you account for it).

Annualized return

annualized = (1 + ROI) ^ (365 / DTE) − 1

A 1% weekly return compounds to ~67% annualized — but only if you keep redeploying capital and avoid catastrophic assignments.

Chain P&L (across rolls)

chain_pnl = Σ premiums_collected − Σ debits_paid_to_close ± realized_stock_pnl

Your tracker organizes by Chain ID for exactly this reason — every roll and the eventual stock exit belong to the same chain.

What Can Go Wrong

Gap-down risk

If a stock gaps down 20% on earnings or news, your put is deep ITM and you eat the full loss minus premium. Premium income from a year can vanish in one bad assignment. Mitigation: avoid earnings weeks unless you've sized for it, diversify across uncorrelated names, size each chain to a small fraction of total capital.

The "stuck bag holder" problem

After assignment, if the stock keeps dropping, every CC strike above your basis pays almost nothing. You're stuck holding a losing position selling tiny premiums. Mitigation: only wheel stocks you'd hold long-term anyway; consider selling CCs below basis if you accept the realized loss.

Capped upside on rallies

If you finally sell a CC and the stock rips 30%, you sell at the strike and miss the rally. The wheel is an income strategy, not a growth strategy.

Tax drag (US accounts)

Short options closed in < 1 year are short-term capital gains. High turnover = high ordinary-income tax. Consider running the wheel in tax-advantaged accounts when possible.

Glossary

CSP — Cash-Secured Put

Short put backed by cash equal to strike × 100. Income trade; obligates you to buy 100 shares at the strike if assigned.

CC — Covered Call

Short call backed by 100 shares you already own. Income trade; obligates you to sell 100 shares at the strike if called away.

PCS — Put Credit Spread

Defined-risk variant of the CSP: sell a put, buy a further-OTM put for protection. Less premium but capped downside. Used in your wheel_advisor app.

Strike

The agreed-upon price at which the option can be exercised.

Premium

The cash the option buyer pays the seller, quoted per share. Multiply by 100 for the per-contract dollar amount.

DTE — Days to Expiration

How many calendar days remain until the option expires. Most wheel trades use 7-45 DTE.

Delta

How much the option price moves per $1 move in the stock. Also a rough proxy for probability of finishing ITM.

IV — Implied Volatility

The market's expected annualized volatility priced into the option. Higher IV = higher premium for the same strike/DTE.

OTM / ATM / ITM

Out / At / In The Money. For a put: OTM = strike below spot. For a call: OTM = strike above spot. ITM = the opposite.

Theta

The daily premium decay of an option, all else equal. As an option seller, theta is your friend.

Roll

A single combined order that closes the current short option and opens a new one, usually for a net credit and a later expiration.

Chain ID

In your tracker, the identifier that ties all legs (opens + rolls + close/assignment) of one logical wheel position together. e.g. TSLA-001.

Block

In your tracker, one block = one option contract = 100 shares of underlying obligation.
